### Section E: Who Said to Whom

**1.** "What use are you to me?"

- **Answer:** This was said by **Robinson Crusoe** to the **gold and silver coins** he found in one of the wooden boxes on the ship. He said this because the coins were of no use to him on the deserted island, where there was no one to trade with and nothing to buy.

**2.** "Everyone in the world desires you."

- **Answer:** This was spoken **about the gold and silver coins.** Crusoe said this to show that while the whole world values gold and silver, they were completely useless to him on the island. This tells us that the speaker felt a deep sense of helplessness and loneliness — being stranded on an island where even the most desired things in the world had no value.

### Section F: Short Answer Questions

**a.** Who was Daniel Defoe? What kind of writing did he do before Robinson Crusoe?

- **Answer:** Daniel Defoe was an English writer born in 1660. His father was a butcher. Before writing Robinson Crusoe, he worked at a number of trades and then began writing at the age of forty. He wrote **poems** and occasionally wrote **criticisms of the government.** Sometimes he was even sent to prison for his writings.

**b.** What happened about twelve days after Robinson Crusoe started his voyage?

- **Answer:** About twelve days into the voyage, a **violent tornado came from the north.** The waves rose to the skies, the sky turned black, and the wind howled through the sails. Two members of the crew were washed overboard and the ship began to leak.

**c.** How did Crusoe manage to reach the shore after the boat capsized?

- **Answer:** When a gigantic wave overturned the small boat, Robinson Crusoe, being a good swimmer, battled in the stormy sea for **an hour and a half.** Again and again huge waves carried him back out to sea. At last, he was **hurled towards the shore** by a wave and fell down exhausted on the sand.

**d.** Where did Robinson Crusoe sleep on his first night on the island, and why did he choose that place?

- **Answer:** On his first night, Crusoe **climbed up a tree and slept between two great branches.** He chose the tree because the night was dark without a star in the sky, and lying on the sand would have been dangerous. He also did not know what wild animals lived in the forest, so he climbed the tree to stay safe.

**e.** What did Robinson Crusoe do when he woke up in the morning after the shipwreck?

- **Answer:** When Crusoe woke up, he saw the ship lying a **quarter of a mile away** from the shore. He came down the tree, found a stream and drank some cool fresh water. He lay down and slept again. When he woke up again with the sun high in the sky, he **resolved to swim out to the ship** and bring back useful things.

**f.** Name any five things that Robinson Crusoe took from the ship.

- **Answer:** Robinson Crusoe took the following things from the ship: **Bread, rice, cheese and wine** (food and drink), **Clothes**, **Two guns and a pair of pistols**, **Powder and shot (ammunition)**, **Tools, wood, rope, nails and sails**, **Gold and silver coins**, **Sugar, flour and bread.**

**g.** How did Robinson Crusoe discover that he was living on an island?

- **Answer:** The next morning after his arrival, Crusoe **walked along the shore and climbed up a little hill.** From the top of the hill, he could see all around and discovered that he was **completely surrounded by water** — he was on an island. He felt like a prisoner without a friend in the world.

**h.** What are some of the tasks Robinson Crusoe performed to survive on the island?

- **Answer:** Robinson Crusoe performed many tasks to survive on the island. He **built a house with a strong stockade** around it using tree trunks and sails. He **made a small boat** and learned to sail it. He **taught himself to make pots** out of mud. He **grew corn, rice and wheat** and taught himself to **make bread.** He also **explored the island** and tamed a baby goat.

**i.** What natural resources did Crusoe find on the island?

- **Answer:** On the island, Crusoe found a **fresh water stream**, **wild grapes and limes** in a valley, **wild goats** in the forest, and **trees** from which he got wood to build his house and raft. The island also had **forests** with wild animals and **fertile land** where he could grow crops.

**j.** Why did Robinson Crusoe not want the gold coins? Do you think they would have come in useful?

- **Answer:** Robinson Crusoe did not want the gold coins because on a **deserted island there was no one to trade with and nothing to buy.** He said, "What use are you to me?" However, the coins could have come in useful **if he were ever rescued** and returned to civilization, as he could have used them to buy goods or start a new life.

**k.** What happened to the ship in the end?

- **Answer:** When Robinson Crusoe returned to his island after one of his trips to the ship, he noticed that the sky had turned black with storm clouds. He saw the ship **rise up on a huge wave and then sink beneath it.** The ship was finally destroyed by the storm and sank into the sea.

**l.** Describe how Crusoe built his home on the island.

- **Answer:** During the next few months, Crusoe busied himself in **making a house with a strong stockade** around it. He **used one of the sails from the ship as the roof.** He **cut down tree trunks and carried them** to build the stockade. The stockade was built strongly enough to **keep out wild animals.** He worked very hard every day to complete his shelter.

**8.** What are monosexual flowers? Give two examples.

**9.** What is the function of the stigma and the style in a pistil?

**10.** What is the role of the ovary and ovules after fertilization?

**11.** What are pollen grains? What do they contain?

**12.** Describe the process of pollination briefly.

- **Answer:** When the anthers of a flower mature, they **split open** and release the pollen grains. These pollen grains are then **carried by agents** like wind, water, insects or birds to the stigma of the same or another flower of the same kind. When the pollen grains land on the **sticky stigma,** pollination is complete. This is the first step towards the formation of seeds and fruits in a plant.

### Section F: Short Answer Questions

**1.** What is fertilization? How does it occur in plants?

- **Answer:** **Fertilization** is the process in which the **male and female gametes fuse together** to form a fertilized cell called the **zygote.** In plants, when a pollen grain lands on the stigma, it germinates and sends out a **pollen tube** through the style. The pollen tube carries the **male gamete** to the **ovule** in the ovary, where it combines with the **female gamete (egg)** to form the zygote.

**2.** What is germination? What conditions are necessary for germination of a seed?

- **Answer:** **Germination** is the process by which a **seed grows into a young plant or seedling.** The conditions necessary for germination are **adequate light, water, air (oxygen), and warmth.** When a seed receives all these conditions, the seed coat breaks and the young plant comes out, developing roots and a shoot.

**3.** What is seed dispersal? Why is it important for the parent plant and new plants?

- **Answer:** **Seed dispersal** is the process by which seeds are **scattered away from the parent plant.** It is important because if seeds do not get dispersed, they will grow very close to the parent plant and **compete for light, air, space, water and nutrients,** and many would die. Seed dispersal ensures that seeds grow in different places, giving both the parent plant and new plants a better chance of survival.

**4.** What is vegetative reproduction? Give two examples.

- **Answer:** **Vegetative reproduction** (or vegetative propagation) is the process in which **new plants grow not only from seeds, but also from other parts** of the plant such as stems, leaves and roots. Two examples are: **Potato** — new plants grow from the eyes (buds) on the stem; **Bryophyllum** — new plants grow from the buds along the margins of its leaves.

**5.** Describe how seeds are dispersed by wind with two examples of plants.

- **Answer:** Seeds dispersed by wind are generally **light in weight** and have special structures to help them float. **Dandelion seeds** have hair-like structures that act like a **parachute,** allowing them to float in the air. **Maple seeds** have **wing-like structures** that help the wind carry them away from the parent plant to new locations.



**6.** Describe how seeds are dispersed by animals with two examples.

- **Answer:** Animals help in dispersing seeds in two ways. Some animals and birds eat fruits and **throw away the seeds,** which then grow into new plants. Some seeds have **hooks, stiff hair or spines** that stick to the bodies of animals. For example, **cocklebur seeds** have hooks that attach to animal fur and are carried away. **Squirrels** eat nuts and the seeds pass through their bodies or are carried to new places.

**7.** What is the structure of a seed? Name its parts and state their functions.

- **Answer:** A seed has two main parts: the **seed coat** and the **cotyledons.** The **seed coat** is the outer covering that protects the young plant inside the seed. **8.** What is the difference between pollination and fertilization?

- **Answer:** **Pollination** is the transfer of pollen grains from the **anther to the stigma** — it is an **external process** that does not directly involve the fusion of gametes. **Fertilization** is the **internal process** within the ovule where the male gamete from the pollen grain **fuses with the female egg,** leading to the formation of the zygote. Pollination must take place before fertilization can occur.

**9.** How do potato plants reproduce vegetatively?

**10.** Why do all seeds not grow into new plants? Give two reasons.

- **Answer:** All seeds do not grow into new plants because: first, **some seeds are eaten by animals** and do not get a chance to germinate; second, **some seeds get insufficient air, water, or warmth** and therefore cannot germinate properly. Some seeds also get destroyed by rain or wind.



**11.** Describe the stages of germination of a seed briefly.

- **Answer:** The stages of germination are: first, the seed receives **water, light and air;** second, the **seed coat breaks** and the young plant comes out; third, the young plant **develops roots and a shoot;** fourth, **tiny leaves develop** on the shoot and start making food; fifth, the **seed shrinks and disappears** as the seedling grows into a new plant.

**12.** What happens to the different parts of a flower after fertilization?

The **ovule** becomes the **seed** and the **ovary** becomes the **fruit.**

**13.** What are cotyledons? What is their function in a seed?

- **Answer:** **Cotyledons** are the seed leaves present inside a seed. Seeds may have one or two cotyledons. Their main function is to **store food** for the young plant during the early stages of germination, before the seedling can make its own food through its leaves.

**14.** How do pea and balsam seeds disperse? What is this type of dispersal called?

- **Answer:** Pea and balsam fruits **burst open forcefully** when they dry. This causes the seeds to be **thrown out** and fall on the ground away from the parent plant. This type of dispersal is called **dispersal by explosion of fruit.**

**15.** What are spores? Name two plants that reproduce through spores.

### Section E: Define the Following Terms

**1. Abacus** — Abacus is one of the first calculating devices, designed to perform simple calculations such as addition, subtraction and multiplication. It was originally made with beans or stones on wooden tablets, later replaced by wooden bamboo frames with beads sliding on wires. It has two sections — Heaven and Earth.

**2. Pascaline** — Pascaline is the first mechanical calculator, invented by French mathematician Blaise Pascal in the 17th century. It could perform addition and subtraction by turning dials and gears.

**3. Difference Engine** — The Difference Engine was a machine built by Charles Babbage in 1822 to compute mathematical tables. It consisted of two major parts — the calculating mechanism and the printing and control mechanism. It was the first complete design for an automatic calculator.

**4. Analytical Engine** — The Analytical Engine was a proposed mechanical general-purpose computer designed by Charles Babbage. It was first described in 1837 and is considered the forerunner of modern computers.

**5. Tabulating Machine** — The Tabulating Machine was a mechanical device developed by Herman Hollerith in the late 19th century. It was used to process and analyse data from the 1890 US Census using punched cards as input.

**6. Supercomputer** — Supercomputers are the most powerful and fastest computers available. They are used for complex scientific calculations, simulations, weather forecasting and other tasks that require immense processing power. Examples: PARAM Siddhi, AIRAWAT.

**7. Mainframe Computer** — Mainframe computers are large, high-performance computers designed for handling extensive data processing tasks and serving multiple users simultaneously. They are commonly used in large organisations such as banks and universities. Examples: IBM 360, IBM 704.

**8. Minicomputer** — Minicomputers (or midrange computers) are smaller and less powerful than mainframes. They bridge the gap between large mainframes and smaller microcomputers. They are more affordable and suitable for multi-user environments. Examples: PDP-8, VAX series.

**9. Microcomputer** — Microcomputers (or Personal Computers) are the computers most commonly used by individuals and small businesses. They include desktops and laptops and are used for tasks ranging from word processing to gaming. Examples: Desktop, Laptop, Tablet.

**10. Vacuum Tube** — A vacuum tube was the main electronic component used in first-generation computers (1940–1956). It was used to process data, but vacuum tubes were large, expensive, produced a lot of heat and consumed a lot of electricity.

**11. Transistor** — A transistor replaced the vacuum tube in second-generation computers (1956–1963). Transistors were smaller, faster, cheaper and produced less heat than vacuum tubes.

**12. Integrated Circuit** — An Integrated Circuit (IC) replaced transistors in third-generation computers (1964–1971). ICs increased the speed and efficiency of computers and made them smaller and cheaper.

**13. Heuristics** — Heuristics is the ability to learn from past mistakes. Unlike humans, a computer cannot learn from experience — it has no heuristics. This is one of the limitations of computers.

**14. Generation of Computers** — A generation of computers refers to a stage in the development of computers, categorised based on the technology used. There are five generations of computers, each using a different technology — from vacuum tubes to Artificial Intelligence.

**15. Evolution of Computers** — The evolution of computers refers to the gradual development of computers over time — from early counting devices like the abacus to modern supercomputers and AI-based machines. Each step in this evolution brought faster, smaller and more powerful computers.

### Section E: Define the Following Terms

**1. Weather** — Weather is the state of the atmosphere for a short period of time — it may change hour to hour, day to day or over a few months. It is generally for a small area and affects our day-to-day lives. It can be predicted.

**2. Climate** — Climate is the average of the data of weather conditions collected over **30 years.** It is an expected condition rather than the actual condition and applies to a large part of the globe. For example, May and June are generally warm in India.

**3. Altitude** — Altitude is the height of a place above sea level. The higher we go, the cooler it gets, as the air at higher altitudes is thinner and absorbs less heat.

**4. Insolation** — Insolation is the amount of light (heat energy) received from the Sun reaching a particular area. Places near the equator receive direct sunrays and have high insolation, while polar regions receive slanted rays and have low insolation.

**5. Humidity** — Humidity is the amount of water vapour present in the atmosphere. More clouds are formed when there is more humidity. Equatorial regions have high humidity, while deserts have very low humidity.

**6. Equable climate** — Equable climate is a moderate, stable climate that does not change suddenly. Cities closer to seas and oceans have equable climate because water heats and cools more slowly than land, keeping temperatures moderate in both summer and winter.

**7. Maritime influence** — Maritime influence refers to the effect of the sea on the climate of nearby coastal places. Because sea temperature stays higher in winter, coastal cities get warmer breezes from the sea in winter, and have lower summer temperatures than inland cities.

**8. Hibernate** — Hibernate means to spend the winter sleeping or in a resting state. Some animals in the temperate regions, like bears, hibernate through winters when food is scarce and temperatures are low.

**9. Migrate** — Migrate means to move from one place to another, usually in search of food, warmer climate or breeding grounds. Some birds like the Siberian crane migrate from temperate regions to warmer places during winter.

**10. Torrid Zone** — The Torrid Zone is the major heat zone that lies between the Tropic of Cancer (23.5°N) and the Tropic of Capricorn (23.5°S). It gets vertical sunrays throughout the year, making it very hot. It is also known as the tropical region and contains thick evergreen rain forests, hot deserts and grasslands.

**11. Temperate Zone** — The Temperate Zone lies between the Tropic of Cancer and the Arctic Circle in the north, and the Tropic of Capricorn and the Antarctic Circle in the south. It has a moderate climate — warm in summer, cold in winter and moderate in spring and autumn. Large forests, grasslands (Prairies and Steppes) and diverse wildlife are found here.

**12. Frigid Zone** — The Frigid Zone is found in the Arctic Circle (66.5°N to the North Pole) and the Antarctic Circle (66.5°S to the South Pole). It is also known as the polar region. It receives very slanted sunrays, has very low insolation and extremely cold temperatures. It is scarcely populated.

**13. Aurora Borealis** — Aurora Borealis is a very beautiful natural phenomenon that occurs in the night sky of the **northern polar (frigid) region.** Bands of green and red lights light up the sky and change their forms. The same phenomenon in the southern polar region is called **Aurora Australis.**

**14. Parched** — Parched means dried out with heat. In the context of weather, it refers to land that has become very dry due to lack of rainfall and intense heat.

**15. Scarcely** — Scarcely means almost not; very little or barely. The frigid zone is scarcely populated because of its extremely cold and harsh climate.

**16. Atmosphere** — The atmosphere is the layer of air that surrounds the Earth. It is held in place by the Earth's gravity and acts as a protective blanket. The Sun keeps the atmosphere — our weather machine — in motion.

### Section F: Short Answer Questions

**1.** List any four elements of weather and climate.

- **Answer:** Four elements that are common to both weather and climate are: **temperature, humidity, rainfall,** and **wind.** Other elements include **air pressure** and **cloud cover.**

**2.** Name the deserts of the world found in the tropical region.

- **Answer:** The deserts found in the tropical (torrid) region are the **Sahara Desert** in Africa, the **Great Victoria Desert** in Australia, and the **Arabian Desert** in the Middle East.

**3.** Which region is also known as the polar region? Where is it located?

- **Answer:** The **Frigid Zone** is also known as the **polar region.** It is located in the **Arctic Circle (66.5°N to the North Pole)** in the north and the **Antarctic Circle (66.5°S to the South Pole)** in the south. This region receives very little sunlight and has extremely cold temperatures.

**4.** Write the names and degrees of the latitudes which mark the limits of the temperate zone in the northern hemisphere.

- **Answer:** In the northern hemisphere, the temperate zone is marked by the **Tropic of Cancer at 23.5°N** (its southern boundary) and the **Arctic Circle at 66.5°N** (its northern boundary).

**5.** Why are most of the temperate forests found in the northern hemisphere?

- **Answer:** Most of the temperate forests are found in the **northern hemisphere** because **very little land area** is present in the southern hemisphere beyond **30°S latitude.** The forests in the temperate zone occupy large areas of **North America, Europe and Asia,** all of which are in the northern hemisphere. Only some parts of New Zealand, Chile and Tasmania have temperate forests in the south.



**6.** How does altitude affect the temperature of a place?

- **Answer:** **Altitude** is the height of a place above sea level. The higher we go, the **cooler it gets.** This is because most of the atmosphere containing water vapour and gases is found closer to the Earth's surface, where they absorb more heat. At higher altitudes, the air is **thinner** and absorbs **less heat,** causing the temperature to drop. That is why people go to the hills during summers as higher altitudes are cooler than the plains.

**7.** Write any three features of the torrid zone.

- **Answer:** Three features of the torrid zone are: first, it lies **between the Tropic of Cancer (23.5°N) and the Tropic of Capricorn (23.5°S)** and receives **vertical sunrays throughout the year,** making it very hot; second, regions 5° to 10° north and south of the equator have **thick evergreen rain forests** due to heavy rainfall; third, some of the **largest hot deserts** in the world — such as the Sahara (Africa), Great Victoria (Australia) and Arabian Desert (Middle East) — are part of the torrid zone.

**8.** Why do places in the frigid zone receive very little sunlight for six months of a year?

- **Answer:** Places in the frigid zone receive very little sunlight for six months because of the **tilt of the Earth's axis (23.5°).** During winter in the polar regions, the Sun is so far below the horizon that its rays barely reach these areas. The sunrays are **very slanted,** travel a longer distance, and are spread over a large area — resulting in **low insolation** and very little warmth. During this period, these places experience continuous darkness, known as the **polar night.**

**9.** How do humidity and cloud cover affect the climate of a place?

- **Answer:** **Humidity** is the amount of water vapour in the atmosphere. When humidity is high, **more clouds are formed,** which can lead to **heavy rainfall.** Equatorial regions get maximum sunshine but also receive heavy rainfall, giving them a large cloud cover. Areas with **low humidity,** such as deserts, have **smaller cloud cover** and experience **intense heat** during the day because clouds are not there to block the sunrays. Thus, humidity and cloud cover directly affect the temperature and rainfall of a place.

**10.** What is the difference between weather and climate? Give two points.

- **Answer:** Two differences between weather and climate are: first, **weather** is the state of the atmosphere for a **short period** (hours, days or months), while **climate** is the **average** of weather conditions collected over **30 years;** second, weather is generally for a **small area** and changes frequently, while climate applies to a **large part of the globe** and is more stable and predictable.



**11.** Why does temperature decrease as we move from the equator to the poles?

- **Answer:** Temperature decreases as we move from the equator to the poles because at the equator, the **Sun's rays strike the surface vertically** (directly), concentrating more heat in a small area. As we move towards the poles, the Earth's surface is **curved,** so the Sun's rays become **more slanted.** Slanted rays travel a **longer distance** through the atmosphere and are **spread over a larger area,** delivering less heat. Hence, places near the poles receive **less insolation** and have lower temperatures.

**12.** Why do coastal cities like Mumbai have a moderate climate compared to inland cities like Nagpur?

- **Answer:** Coastal cities like Mumbai have a moderate climate because of **maritime influence.** Water heats and cools **more slowly** than land. So in summer, the sea keeps the coastal city **cooler** than inland cities, and in winter, the sea remains warmer and sends **warm breezes** to the coast, keeping it warmer than inland areas. Nagpur, being an inland location far from the sea, experiences **extreme temperatures** — very hot summers and colder winters — as it lacks the moderating effect of the sea.

**13.** What is Aurora Borealis? Where is it seen?

- **Answer:** **Aurora Borealis** is a beautiful natural phenomenon that occurs in the **night sky of the northern polar (frigid) region.** It appears as bands of **green and red lights** that light up the sky and keep changing their forms. It is also known as the **Northern Lights.** The same phenomenon in the **southern polar region** is called **Aurora Australis** (or the Southern Lights).
